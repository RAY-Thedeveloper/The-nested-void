THE NESTED VOID
Being the Last Writings of Sundaram Pandey, Student
Assembled in the order they were found
✦ ✦ ✦
What follows was recovered from Room 214, BPXXMT Men's Hostel, on the morning of 22nd March 2026. The pages were not found in order. We have assembled them as best we could. We are not certain we have assembled them correctly. We are not certain it matters. — The Editorial Committee, Axxom Literary Magazine.

The first thing you should understand is that we did not find a body.

The second thing you should understand is that we are not sure whether that is better or worse.

His diary was open on the desk. His phone was face-down beside it. The novel he had been reading — a black-spined, unmarked thing, no author, no publisher listed — was open on the bed, and beside that novel was a second, smaller book, its pages damp at the edges as though they had been breathed on. No one could explain the second book. No one had seen it before. No one could read the title on its cover, because the title kept appearing to be something different each time you looked at it directly.

We are printing what he wrote. We are printing all of it.

We were warned not to.

We are printing it anyway, because we think — we hope — that understanding requires witnesses. That stories need readers the way lungs need air.

We may be wrong about this.

Fragment I — The Diary of Sundaram Pandey — undated, found loose
[ written in ink, pressed hard enough to leave impressions on the page beneath ] Don't think. Don't say.
Don't think, don't say.
Don't think. Don't think. Don't say.

If someone is reading this, by the time you are reading this I will be dead.

My name is Sundaram Pandey. I am a student of BPXXMT. It all started on 8th March 2026 when my friend Souvik sent a WhatsApp message asking for write-ups for the college magazine — Axxom. I was short on MAR points. I was desperate. I applied. From there, my world changed.

I don't know what is real anymore.

For those of you who will read this, I have only one thing to say:

Do not believe it. As long as you do not believe it, nothing will happen to you.

I believed it.
I believed every word of it.
That was my mistake.

I keep writing that paragraph and scratching it out and writing it again. It has become a ritual. The way religious people say prayers they've stopped understanding — the words worn smooth like river stones, still necessary, still said. Maybe writing it is the only thing keeping me on this side. Maybe once I stop writing it, I'll slip through entirely, the way a bookmark slips from a novel left in the rain.

I don't know what "this side" means anymore. That's the problem.

Let me start, as I always do, with the book.

Fragment IV — Diary — 9th March 2026, 11:14 PM

The One I Was Reading When Everything Started

I have always been a reader. My mother said I was born holding a book — which is biologically impossible but emotionally accurate. My room here at the hostel is impossible to navigate after dark because the floor is a continent of stacked paperbacks. Vishal says it's a fire hazard. I tell him the books would protect me. He doesn't find this funny. I still believe it.

On the evening of 7th March — the night before Souvik's message — I was reading a novel I had found tucked behind the radiator in the hostel common room. No title on the spine. No author. The cover was plain black cloth, soft as old skin. I assumed some fourth-year had left it. I assumed it was nothing.

The novel was called — or at least it seemed to be called, on the first interior page — The Man Who Read Himself to Sleep.

Its protagonist was a man named Soham. Soham was, in the novel, reading a novel.

Soham had not moved from his chair in four days. This bothered no one because no one had noticed. The curtains were the colour of old bone. The light through them was the colour of a bruise healing the wrong way. His tea had gone cold seven times and he had reheated it and let it go cold again, because the act of reheating was the only thing that reminded him he still had hands.

The novel in his lap was called — or seemed to be called, the title shifting in his peripheral vision like something seen through water — The Cartography of Unmade Rooms. Its protagonist was a woman named Soumalli. Soumalli was reading a novel.

Soumalli had found the book under her mattress. She did not remember putting it there. She was reasonably certain she had not. The cover was the colour of dried blood on slate. Inside, the story was about a man named Supratik.

Supratik was lazy. This is the most honest thing that can be said about him. He was spectacularly, architecturally lazy — lazy in the way of old monuments, majestic in his stillness, eroded only at the edges by the weak weather of obligation. He had not contributed meaningfully to any plot he had ever found himself in. He drifted. He observed. He ate whatever was left on the stove. He was, in the technical sense, a protagonist, but he wore the title the way a scarecrow wears clothes: present but unconvincing.

The only interesting thing about Supratik was that every morning he woke with the absolute certainty that someone, somewhere, was reading about him. He could never shake the feeling of being watched from a direction that had no name.

He found this feeling easier to live with than to investigate.

So he didn't.

He went back to sleep.

Soumalli read this and laughed and then stopped laughing for reasons she couldn't articulate. She put the book face-down on the pillow and went to the window. The street below was normal. The sky above was normal. She told herself she was being ridiculous. She picked the book back up. Supratik was still asleep in it. She felt, reading him, an envy she wasn't prepared to examine.

I read this passage and I stopped. I reread it. I went to the window of my room. The campus below was normal. The sky above was normal. I told myself I was being ridiculous. I went back to the book.

Soham was still in his chair. The tea was cold again.

I slept with the book on my chest, face-up. This was, I think, my first mistake. But how do you know which mistake is the first? You only see them in sequence after the last one, and by then the sequencing has stopped mattering.

Fragment II — Diary — 8th March 2026, exact time unclear, ink smeared

The Message

I woke to the sound of my phone. Not a ring — a notification. That soft, specific chime of WhatsApp, like a small stone dropped in still water. Three AM. Or possibly eleven AM. My curtains were thick and my sense of time had become unreliable since I started the book.

8 Mar, 11:07 AM Souvik 🔱

Hello everyone, We are collecting write-ups for the upcoming CSE Department Magazine. Students who enjoy writing are invited to submit their poems, short stories, articles, or any creative pieces. If you would like your work to be featured, please send your submissions soon. Selected entries will be included in the magazine. Looking forward to your creative contributions.

link: [Axxom.BPXXMT.in/submit/2026]

MAR points. The institutional terror of every BPXXMT student — those mandatory participation credits that stood between you and academic progression, between you and dignity, between you and the vague bureaucratic approval that passed for a future here. I was short. I knew I was short. I had been avoiding knowing I was short by reading novels, which is, I recognize, a pattern.

I stared at the message for a long time. Then I looked at the black-spined novel on my pillow. Soham was inside it, in his chair, his tea cold. Soumalli was inside that, at her window. Supratik was inside that, asleep, certain he was being watched.

I had the strange, vertiginous thought: what if I write about this? About the nested books. About the feeling they gave me. About Supratik, who felt watched but chose not to look.

I submitted the form. I titled my piece The Nested Void.

I should not have given it that name. Names are not neutral. They are invitations. They are addresses.

Fragment VII — Diary — date illegible, written in a different pen

The Repetitions

Something I have noticed — I don't know when I first noticed it, the noticing itself seems to come from somewhere in the middle rather than a beginning — is that things have begun to repeat.

Not dramatically. Not like a horror film. Subtly. The way a song gets stuck in your head and you only realize it's been playing when you try to think about something else and find it there, already occupying all the space.

For instance: Souvik's message. I have read it [ MANY ] times. Each time I scroll back to it, the timestamp is different. Not by hours — by minutes. 11:07. Then 11:09. Then 11:02. I showed Vishal. He said the timestamp looked normal to him: 11:07. He didn't understand why I was asking. His face had the specific patience of someone who has decided not to be disturbed by something they find disturbing.

For instance: the radiator in the common room. I went back to look for more books. The radiator was gone. I asked the warden. He said there had never been a radiator in the common room. I remembered leaning against it last winter. I remember the specific burn of it against my shoulder blade through my sweatshirt. He said I was perhaps thinking of a different room.

For instance: the book.

Soham closed his eyes and had a terrible thought. The thought was this: what if the book he was reading was reading him back? What if attention was not a one-way street — what if every act of reading was also an act of being witnessed? He had always felt, looking at a page, that the page looked back. He had always dismissed this feeling as the anxiety of a man who spent too much time alone.

He was no longer certain it was anxiety.

He was no longer certain he was alone.

Soumalli turned to page seventy-three and found a sentence she was certain had not been there before:

"Supratik woke that morning with the knowledge that the woman reading about him had a name. He didn't know how he knew this. He didn't know how he knew her name was Soumalli. He didn't know how he knew that Soumalli was being read by a man whose name he almost knew — a name that kept slipping, that felt like a name he had heard in a dream, starting with something like S."

Soumalli read this sentence three times. She put the book down. She picked it back up. The sentence was still there. It knew her name. She told herself: books don't know names. Books are not sentient. Books cannot reach out of their own narrative and point at a reader.

She told herself this, and the book sat quietly in her lap, and Supratik slept on in it, wearing a small smile she was certain had not been on his face before.

Soham read this and understood, with a clarity that felt less like insight and more like water rising in a room, that the nested novels were not nested at all. That this was not a structure. It was a descent.

I read this at 2 AM on a night whose date I have since lost track of. I put the book down. I wrote: books are not sentient. I wrote it twice. I wrote it a third time and noticed that the ink from the second time had not yet dried, which meant either I had written it very recently or time in my room was moving strangely.

I am writing in my diary. I am writing this down.

It occurs to me, as I write — and I do not know how to say this without sounding like I have already lost the argument with my own sanity — that you are reading this. Right now. You, specifically, whoever you are, wherever you are. I am writing in a diary that will become — that has already become, from my position in time — a found document. You are reading a found document. You are reading the interior of my experience the way I am reading Soham's, the way he is reading Soumalli's, the way she is reading Supratik's.

Do you feel watched?

You should.

We all do, at this depth.

Fragment XI — the write-up submitted to Axxom (recovered from submission portal)

THE NESTED VOID

Submitted by: Sundaram Pandey, 3rd Year, CSE
Submitted: 13th March 2026, 3:47 AM
✦

I want to tell you about a man named Soham who is reading a book. Inside that book is a woman named Soumalli who is reading another book. Inside that book is a man named Supratik who is not reading anything because Supratik does not do much of anything — Supratik, the great refuser, the protagonist who sidesteps every plot like a man who knows the plot is a trap.

Supratik knows, at some level, that he is a character. He has always known. It's why he stays still — because he suspects that the story only happens when he moves. So he has remained motionless for as many pages as he can, hoping to starve the narrative of its engine.

It hasn't worked. Soumalli reading him gives him motion. Soham reading her gives Soumalli motion. I reading Soham give all of them motion. And you — you reading this — give me motion.

This is not a metaphor. Or rather, it is a metaphor, but it is also not only a metaphor, which is the most dangerous kind.

Here is what I have come to understand in the fourteen days since I found the book: the worlds inside novels are not sealed. They breathe. They seep. The membrane between the page and the eye is thinner than we pretend, and when you read something — really read it, with your whole attention, with the kind of hungry focus I have given to that black-spined novel — something of you travels inward, and something of it travels outward. An exchange. Osmosis. The terror of the book is not that it is a horror novel. The terror of the book is that it knows what I am, and it has been waiting for someone like me — someone who reads with belief, who forgets to keep one foot in the world of radiators and cold tea and WhatsApp messages — to open it.

Supratik, the lazy one, the still one — Supratik is the smart one. He knew not to engage. He knew the plot was a mouth. He closed his eyes and lay down and refused.

I applied for a magazine.

I opened my mouth. I walked into it.

I am writing to you from inside.

Don't believe it.
As long as you don't believe it, nothing will happen.
I believed it.
[This submission was accepted for publication. Sundaram Pandey did not respond to the acceptance email. The email was sent on 15th March 2026. By 22nd March, his room was empty.]

Fragment IX — Diary — 15th March 2026, or possibly earlier

What The Book Said Next

I have read to page two hundred and eleven. Something has changed at page two hundred and eleven that I need to write down before I forget it, or before it decides I don't need to remember it, which amounts to the same thing from the outside but is a very different catastrophe from the inside.

On page two hundred and eleven of Soham's novel, Soumalli turned to a new chapter and found that Supratik had woken up.

Not the small wakings — not the half-conscious stirrings to reheat food or confirm that the world still existed outside the window. A real waking. Supratik sat upright in his novel and looked directly at Soumalli for the first time in either of their existences and said, in the particular flat voice of a man who has run out of patience with his own cowardice:

"You need to put the book down."

Soumalli said: "You can't talk to me. You're a character. I'm a character. We're in different books."

Supratik said: "That distinction stopped mattering about forty pages ago. The walls got thin. You've been reading me for long enough that you've started believing in me, and believing in me is what gave me a voice, and I'm using that voice to tell you: put the book down. Tell the man who's reading you to put his book down. Tell whoever is reading him—"

He paused. He looked up from inside himself, in the particular direction of someone sensing altitude.

"Tell him," Supratik said quietly, "that writing about it is the same as reading it. That submitting it to a magazine is the same as an open door. That names are addresses. That the title he gave his piece — The Nested Void — was not a title. It was a key. And something has been on the other side of the lock this whole time, waiting for someone to be clever enough and stupid enough and lonely enough to use it."

Soumalli stared at the page. Supratik looked at her with the specific exhaustion of someone who has known something terrible for a very long time and has just now decided to say it out loud.

"I have been lying here," he said, "pretending not to contribute to the plot, because I could see what the plot wanted. I thought if I refused to move, the plot would starve. I was wrong. The plot doesn't need me. It needed someone to read about me refusing to move. It needed the reader."

He looked up again. Past Soumalli. Past Soham. Into a direction that had no name.

"Hello," Supratik said. "You've been very patient. I'm sorry I can't be more useful. I tried to save you by doing nothing. It didn't work. I'm sorry."

I read this and sat very still for a long time.

Supratik had looked up past two layers of fiction and spoken, in the direction of a direction that has no name, to someone reading a diary written by a boy in a hostel room who found a book behind a radiator that the warden says never existed.

He looked at me. He looked past me.

He looked at you.

I put the book down.

I could still hear him.

Fragment XIII — Diary — final entry, found open on the desk

22nd March 2026 — or the last entry, in either case

[ written in two different inks, as though interrupted and resumed ] I know how this looks. I know what "I found a magic book and it ruined my life" sounds like from the outside. I know exactly how it sounds because I am the person who used to read about that kind of person and think: they should have put the book down sooner. They should have been less credulous. Less hungry. Less lonely.

I was all three of those things. I am still all three. The hunger especially.

The thing I need you to understand — the thing I have been circling for all these pages, for all these fragments — is that the book was not supernatural. Or rather: it was, but not in the way you're imagining. It was not haunted. It was not cursed. It was simply constructed in such a way that a person who believed in it completely would, by believing, become part of its structure. The same way Soumalli became part of the innermost book by reading it. The same way Soham became part of the middle book by reading Soumalli. The same way I —

[ the ink changes here. the handwriting is slightly larger. as though written by someone who needs glasses they are not wearing, or by someone very tired, or by someone writing in the dark ]

Are you connecting the dots? I told you the events would be scattered. I told you it would not be simple. Here is what I know: there were three books. There were four layers. The fourth layer was the reader — not me, I was the third — the fourth layer is whoever reads this document. Whoever reads Axxom magazine issue March 2026. Whoever was given this story by someone who found it, or stumbled on it, or was sent it by a friend who thought it was just a weird piece of creative writing.

You are the fourth layer. You are where Supratik was looking.

He told you to put the book down. I am telling you the same thing. Finish this sentence and stop. Do not try to understand the full shape of what you've read. Do not go looking for the black-spined novel — not that you could, not that it exists on any shelf that has a fixed address, but if you go looking with enough hunger you may find that it finds you instead.

I am not dead yet as I write this. I say "I will be dead by the time you read this" because I know the logic now. I know how this kind of story works. I know that I am past the event horizon — that I have believed too completely, submitted the key, said the name aloud in print — and that the direction with no name has noticed me the way the deep ocean notices a stone: with absolute indifference and absolute inevitability.

I am not frightened. I was, for a while. But Supratik is not frightened either, and I have spent enough time inside his world that some of his stillness has migrated into me. He was lazy. He was smart. He refused to move and it saved no one but at least it was a kind of dignity.

I chose to move. I applied. I wrote. I named it.

At least I chose.

Don't think. Don't say.
Don't think, don't say.
If you are still reading — stop reading.
Close this. Close whatever window this is in.
Do not wonder what happened to me.
Do not go looking for the radiator.
Do not try to find the book.
Do not name the thing you felt when you read the part where Supratik looked up.

It already knows your name. It has known since you started.

The only protection is disbelief.

I believed.

— Sundaram Pandey, BPXXMT, final entry

Editor's Closing Note — Axxom Magazine, March 2026

We printed this piece as-is. We told ourselves it was good fiction. We told ourselves the boy had taken a leave of absence. We told ourselves that the black-spined book recovered from his room was a prop — a handmade thing, a project for some course — and that the interior text, which none of us could fully agree on having read, was simply an elaborate piece of metafiction.

We told ourselves: it is only a story. Stories don't hurt anyone. Stories don't move through the air like weather. Stories are not structures you can fall into and not come back from.

We are still telling ourselves these things.

We notice, in our quieter moments, that the things we are telling ourselves are the same things Soumalli told herself. We notice that we are cataloguing our reassurances the way Soham catalogued his cold teas — compulsively, ritualistically, in place of looking at the thing we don't want to look at.

We notice that Sundaram told us not to publish this. We published it anyway because we said: it is only a story.

We are beginning to wonder if that was our mistake. We are beginning to wonder if naming the thing you read — discussing it, passing it to friends, putting it in a magazine that circulates through a campus and then beyond, into the hands of people who like mysteries — is how the fourth layer becomes a fifth. And the fifth becomes a sixth. And the depth keeps going, and the direction with no name gets crowded.

Sundaram said: as long as you don't believe it, nothing will happen.

We are not asking you to believe it.

We are only asking you to consider: at what point in the last several pages did you forget to disbelieve?

We can't remember when we did.

We've looked, and we can't find the moment. It was subtle. It arrived the way cold arrives in a room where the window has been open all day — not as an event but as a condition, already established by the time you notice.

Supratik is still in the book. Soumalli is still in the book. Soham is still in his chair. Sundaram is — Sundaram is wherever the fourth layer goes.

You are reading this in the fifth layer now.

We think you knew that already.

We think you knew that before you even started.
